## Key Takeaways

### Yuval's Definition of MVP
- MVP is NOT just "build something quick and dirty"
- Must answer two core questions with clear data:
  1. **Usability (Can they use it?)** - Real usability testing, not assumptions
  2. **Value (Would they pay?)** - NPS-style questions to validate perceived value

### Marty Cagan's 5 MVP Questions
Yuval references Marty Cagan's framework (his mentor through Adia Roka at Klarna):

1. **Could they use it?** (Usability) - Can users actually navigate and complete tasks?
2. **Would they use it?** (Value) - Do they see enough value to engage?
3. **Can we build it?** (Technical Feasibility) - Can this scale beyond prototype?
4. **Is it financially viable?** (Unit Economics) - Does the math work at scale?
5. **Should we do it?** (Ethics) - Is it ethical? Are we exploiting users' data?

### Technical Feasibility Warning
- If you build something "brute force" (ברוק ובוץ) that can't be turned into a real product at scale, you have a problem
- If your MVP is 100% manual/human service and you say "we'll automate later" - that's NOT an MVP, that's **Wishful Thinking**
- If technology is a critical component, it MUST be part of the MVP
- You need at least a strong hypothesis for how to scale it technically

### Pricing as Part of MVP
- Giving something away for free doesn't validate MVP
- Willingness to pay is a FEATURE of the product
- Only when you add pricing do you know if it's truly viable
- For consumer products: 50-100 paying customers = MVP validated

### RiseUp's MVP Journey
1. **Problem validation first:** Went door-to-door in neighborhoods, pitched on WhatsApp
2. **Discovery:** People in bankruptcy recovery felt MORE in control of money (forced constraints)
3. **Insight:** Borrowed from Fitbit's step counter - created ONE simple number for weekly spending
4. **First MVP was too minimal:** Users saw the number but didn't trust it
5. **Learning:** Users needed to "drill down" into the data to build trust before relying on the single number
6. **Key insight:** "We needed to deepen the product to simplify it" - counterintuitive but critical
7. **Analytics:** Early data was literally Excel spreadsheets, manually counting like a calendar
8. **Scale:** 150 customers before building real automation

### Data Strategy
- Must have a data strategy even at MVP stage
- With 50-100 customers, you MUST combine quantitative data with customer interviews
- "What you see in the data is not always what the customer experiences"
- Customer interviews at MVP stage are CRITICAL - every team that skips this struggles

### On Fundraising & MVP
- Build as much as possible BEFORE talking to investors
- Today's no-code tools make it possible to validate without taking a dollar
- Coming to investors with a validated MVP = stronger position, better valuation
- Each of Cagan's 5 questions you can answer = de-risked for investors

### Yuval's Key Quotes
- "MVP is not about building something minimal - it's about finding product-solution fit"
- "100% human service that you'll automate later is not MVP, it's Wishful Thinking"
- "Paying money is part of the product - it's one of the features you must validate"
- "You must master customer interviews - be in the environment, be proficient at picking up the phone"
- "We needed to deepen the product to simplify it" (counterintuitive MVP lesson)
- "Build a new economy - win-win where customers grow with you, not one where you exploit their data for ads"
